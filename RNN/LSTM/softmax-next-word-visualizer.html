Softmax is the last step: the LSTM turns the context into a hidden state h, a Dense layer turns h into one raw score (logit) per vocabulary word, and softmax turns those scores into probabilities.

1What softmax does — scores → probabilities

Softmax takes any list of raw scores (they can be negative, huge, tiny) and squashes them into positive numbers that add up to 1 — a probability distribution. Drag the sliders and watch it work.

softmax(z)i = eziΣj ezj  — exponentiate each score, then divide by the total

The 3 steps: ① a logit can be any number ② ez makes it positive and blows up big scores ③ dividing by the total normalizes everything to add to 1. Bigger logit → bigger share of the probability. (Tip: raising one slider steals probability from the others.)

2Probability of what? — the next word

In next-word prediction there is one logit per word in the vocabulary. Softmax turns them into P(word is next) for every word. They sum to 1, and the model’s prediction is the highest-probability word (⭐).

context: “the cat sat on the ___”
This is the whole answer to “probability of what?”: softmax gives P(next word = mat), P(next word = floor), … across the entire vocabulary. Predicting a word = picking the biggest bar. (7 words here; real models have 30,000–50,000+.)

3What backprop does when learning the next word

Suppose the true next word (from the text) is “mat”. Training should make the model assign it a higher probability. Watch it happen — press Take a gradient step.

Before training, the model may rank another word first. The gradient p − y pushes the true word’s logit up and every other logit down, so P(“mat”) rises and the loss falls.
Where the gradient goes next: p − y is only the first step of backprop (at the logits). From here it flows back through the Dense layer and then through time into the LSTM/RNN (backprop-through-time) to update Wᵢ, Wₕ, Wₒ and the gates — so next time, given this context, the whole network leans toward “mat”.
